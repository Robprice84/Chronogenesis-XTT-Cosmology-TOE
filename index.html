What Is Chronogenesis?

What if time wasn’t just something that ticks — but something that builds the universe? Imagine time as a real, physical field — like a fabric that can stretch, compress, and ripple. What if the reason stars shine, atoms exist, and galaxies form isn’t because of particles flying through space, but because of how time itself twists and curves?

That question led to the birth of Expanse-Time Theory (XTT). It started with a puzzle from the James Webb Space Telescope: galaxies forming far too early — just 300 million years after the Big Bang. According to standard cosmology, they shouldn't have existed yet.

XTT offered a new idea: maybe it’s not just space that expands — time expands too. In this model, early time flowed more slowly, which gave galaxies more "internal time" to form. That one shift resolved the redshift drift problem, fit early galaxy formation, matched cosmic microwave background data — and did it without needing dark energy or inflation.

But as we kept testing XTT, we uncovered something deeper. The expansion of time wasn’t enough. We needed to explain why time could compress, ripple, and bend. That’s when the idea grew into Chronogenesis.

Chronogenesis treats time — represented by a real physical field, Θ(x, t) — as the source of all structure. Its curvature generates mass, energy, gravity, entropy, even quantum behavior. Not from particles or forces in space, but from how time folds across the universe.

This one principle has now allowed us to match:

  • All 17 Standard Model particle masses (with no free parameters)
  • Gravitational wave frequency drift (LIGO, NANOGrav)
  • Redshift behavior and early galaxy formation (JWST, DESI, Planck)
  • Quantum decoherence timing and atomic clock drift (BIPM, Galileo)

We didn’t set out to build a new theory of everything. We followed a question about time — and it led us to Chronogenesis.
